## 1. Core Definition & Strategic Vision

### What is AI Literacy?

**TU Berlin perspective** (Kowalska):

> The intersection of **technical competence, legal/ethical awareness, and critical reflection** in the context of AI.

Three interconnected dimensions:
- **Technical know-how**: understanding how AI systems work, their capabilities and limitations
- **Reflective application**: knowing when and how to use AI appropriately
- **Ecological & societal impact**: understanding resource consumption, environmental footprint, social responsibility

### Strategic Purpose

AI Literacy is **not** about training people to become AI experts. Instead, it's about developing citizens and professionals who can:
- Use AI tools competently and responsibly
- Understand the lifecycle of AI systems (where do they come from, what powers them, what are the environmental costs)
- Make informed decisions about when AI is appropriate vs. when it's not
- Engage critically with AI governance and ethics

**Long-term vision** (Kowalska, TU Berlin): By 2026+, AI Literacy transitions from a project status to **embedded organizational culture**, supporting the university's core mission of excellent teaching, research, and societal responsibility.

## 2. TU Berlin Taskforce Model: Building AI Literacy Systematically

### Context & Origins

- **Initiative**: TU Berlin (40,000+ members: 8,000 staff, 35,000 students)
- **Driver**: KI-LAB 2025 (Hochschulforum Digitalisierung)
- **Timeline**: Strategic rollout throughout 2026
- **Key leaders**: Dr. Katharina Kinga Kowalska (Program Director for AI & Sustainability, CIO Office), Adrian Siebing (Lecturer, New Web Technologies)

### Taskforce Mandate & Structure

**Purpose**: Develop strategic guiding principles (_Leitsätze_) that serve as the foundation for later leitlinien (guidelines) and practical handreichungen (guidance tools).

**Composition** (interdisciplinary, cross-status):
- Research representatives
- Teaching staff (faculty)
- Central administration
- Student representatives
- Staff council (_Personalrat_) delegates
- Data protection officers
- Deliberately designed to bring **different perspectives and surfacing tensions**

### Methodological Approach

**Phase 1: Discovery & Co-Design**
- Conducted interviews with all taskforce members
- Developed initial draft guiding principles (_Leitsätze_)
- Focused on understanding different institutional perspectives

**Phase 2: Tension Resolution (critical step)**
- Initial drafts were **too regulatory** because they were based on existing rule-setting frameworks
- **Conscious pivot**: reoriented toward **positive vision** emphasizing experimentation and innovation culture
- Bundled compliance themes (data protection, IP, labor law) but emphasized the **university's supporting role** in handling complexity
- Strengthened emphasis on experimental spaces and open innovation culture

**Phase 3: Consolidation & Stakeholder Alignment**
- Intensive workshop discussion of each principle
- Prioritization and cross-system review
- Final alignment with broader institutional strategies (digitalization strategy, sustainability goals)
- **Result**: broad consensus around vision-driven, transformation-oriented framing

### Key Success Factors

1. **Transparency**: Open communication about where TU stands, what's missing, what's next
2. **Multiple channels for participation**: emails, info events, intranet, posters, surveys
3. **High participation**: AI surveys generated responses higher than expected (shows strong institutional interest)
4. **Feedback loops**: Published results from AI surveys, explicitly incorporated community input
5. **Systemic thinking**: Strategy, governance, and competence-building treated as integrated system (not separately)

## 3. UZH AI Hexagon Framework

### Definition

**The AI Hexagon** is the UZH AI Competence Hub's 6-dimensional framework for integrating AI into study programs and curricula.

**Six Design Areas**:
1. **Curricular Integration**: How AI integrates into the curriculum structure
2. **Ethics & Governance**: Ethical frameworks and decision-making processes
3. **Disciplinary Alignment**: Discipline-specific applications and approaches
4. **AI-Conscious Assessment**: How to assess students in the presence of AI (exams, assignments)
5. **Progressive Competence Development**: Building competencies incrementally across the program
6. **Didactic Innovation**: Pedagogical approaches to teaching with/about AI

### Institutional Context

- **Owner**: AI Competence Hub (Alexandra, David) at UZH
- **Relationship to AI Buddy**: Complementary
  - AI Competence Hub: faculty-facing, curriculum-driven
  - AI Buddy: student-facing, learning support tool
- **Key deliverable**: "AI Curriculum Construction Site" (modular AI building blocks for BA/MA programs)
- **Current status**: 12 confirmed AI Coach projects, 2 on hold

### Synergy with AI Buddy

Many faculty projects need a chatbot but find that existing tools (Copilot, generic ChatGPT) don't support institutional data integration. This creates a natural entry point for **AI Buddy as an implementation tool** for the AI Hexagon.

## 4. Implementation Approaches: Multi-Level Strategy

### Level 1: Guiding Principles (_Leitsätze_)

**TU Berlin example** (to be defined by end of 2026):
- Emphasize competence + responsibility over restriction
- Balance experimentation with compliance
- Position the university as a supportive facilitator, not just a regulator
- Integrate sustainability and SDG alignment
- Treat AI Literacy as core to university mission (teaching, research, societal responsibility)

### Level 2: Guidelines (_Leitlinien_)

Build operational guidelines based on principles. Examples:
- When is proprietary AI use acceptable vs. institutional infrastructure required?
- How to evaluate tools for GDPR, IP, and ethical compliance?
- Lecturers' responsibilities when using AI in teaching
- Students' rights and responsibilities with AI

### Level 3: Practical Guidance (_Handreichung_)

Concrete tools and checklists:
- **Governance evaluation methodology** (TU Berlin's approach): systematic assessment of whether existing structures can handle AI governance requirements (compliance, data protection, IP, labor law, institutional policies)
- Checklist for tool evaluation (data protection, security, terms of service, IP)
- "AI Roadshow" format: short slots in existing training, presentations in governance bodies
- Decision trees for common scenarios

### Level 4: Embedding in Organizational Culture

**TU Berlin vision**: by 2027-2028, stop talking about "AI Literacy" as a project because it's become normal organizational practice.

Embedding mechanisms:
- Integration into faculty strategic plans
- Links in university intranet and governance documents
- Regular communication and feedback loops
- Monitoring and iterative adjustment

## 5. Governance & Structural Challenges

### Challenge 1: Decentralization vs. Central Coordination

**The tension** (Siebing, TU Berlin):
- Individual departments develop their own AI solutions (decentralization)
- This creates fragmentation, lack of standards, duplicated infrastructure
- Each faculty builds its own hardware for AI research/teaching
- Students end up using proprietary systems (OpenAI, Mistral, Anthropic) because university doesn't provide alternatives

**Why it matters**:
- Students learn bad practices (using cheapest/easiest tool, not most appropriate)
- Unsustainable resource duplication
- Missing opportunity for institutional AI sovereignty
- Limits institutional capacity to set ethical standards

**Possible solution**:
- Central infrastructure that hosts and provides AI resources (while allowing faculty autonomy in use)
- Shared GPU/compute resources managed by university
- Clear governance for tool evaluation and access
- Balance: central platform + decentralized implementation

### Challenge 2: Speed vs. Institutional Capacity

**The tension** (Kowalska):
- Technology moves at enormous speed (new models every few weeks)
- Institutional processes are slow (board meetings, compliance reviews, policy development)
- Staff want to experiment; leadership wants governance clarity
- Balancing act: maintain innovation culture while establishing guardrails

### Challenge 3: Regulatory Complexity

**Dimensions needing governance clarity**:
- Data protection (GDPR, Swiss DPA, institutional policies)
- Intellectual property (who owns work created with AI? student outputs?)
- Labor law (use of AI for HR decisions, performance evaluation)
- Information security (data flows, model endpoints, access control)
- Institutional autonomy (which decisions stay decentralized, which need central approval)

**TU Berlin's approach**:
- Developing a **governance evaluation methodology**: systematic, lightweight checklist to assess whether existing structures (Data Protection Officer, Legal Department, IT Security) can handle a given AI application
- Goal: avoid case-by-case review of every tool/application; instead, establish criteria for self-assessment by faculty

### Challenge 4: Integration with Existing Structures

**Institutional reality**:
- Many AI literacy activities already exist (training programs, faculty initiatives, IT systems)
- New strategy can't replace existing structures; must build *on* them
- Process runs "partly in reverse": best practices already exist, now need to write principles and coherent strategy

## 6. Key Learnings for AI Buddy

### Alignment Points

**1. Positioning AI Buddy as an AI Literacy Tool**

AI Buddy can implement the AI Hexagon for students:
- **Phase 1 (Phase 1 Companion)**: Help students understand how to use AI in their academic journey, guide toward UZH tools (KlickerUZH, EducationAI, OLAT), explain UZH AI policies
- **Phase 2 (OLAT Opt-in Tutoring)**: Lecturers voluntarily enable AI-powered learning support for their courses, providing institution-approved, course-specific content
- **Phase 3 (Learning Analytics)**: Aggregated, anonymized data on student learning needs feeds back to Dekanate and lecturers

This aligns naturally with the UZH AI Hexagon and AI Competence Hub.

**2. Governance Model: Taskforce + Transparency**

Key success factor from TU Berlin:
- **Interdisciplinary taskforce** representing different perspectives
- **Explicit tension resolution**: not avoiding tradeoffs, but explicitly discussing them and choosing a strategic direction
- **Transparency**: communicate clearly what's decided, why, and what's next
- **Systemic thinking**: don't separate strategy, governance, and competence-building

**3. Infrastructure & Decentralization**

Critical insight from Siebing (TU Berlin):
- Universities need to provide **central AI infrastructure** (not just guidelines)
- Students should not default to proprietary tools due to lack of alternatives
- Faculty need reliable, governed access to compute/models
- This enables institutional AI sovereignty and ethical control

For AI Buddy:
- VVZ-MCP, UZH Services-MCP, and future MCP servers are part of this infrastructure layer
- Progressive handover to ZI is aligned with institutional sustainability

**4. Embedding AI Literacy in Curricula**

The AI Hexagon shows that AI literacy is not a single course or intervention, but:
- **Curricular integration** (across programs and semesters)
- **Assessment methods** that account for AI (how do we test learning when students can use AI?)
- **Disciplinary alignment** (how AI applies in each field)
- **Ethics & governance** integrated throughout

AI Buddy's learning support features (flashcards, mock exams, lecture context) fit naturally into this framework.

### Strategic Questions for AI Buddy

1. **How does AI Buddy's Phase 1 (AI Literacy Companion) complement UZH's AI Competence Hub?**
   - Potential synergy: AI Buddy explains UZH policies, guides toward appropriate tools, provides meta-competency

2. **What's our role in UZH's central AI infrastructure?**
   - MCP servers as reusable institutional components
   - RAG-as-a-Service enabling other UZH projects
   - Langfuse analytics as shared observability layer

3. **How does our governance fit into UZH's broader AI Literacy governance?**
   - Are we part of the strategic conversation with VP EDU, AI Competence Hub, Dekanate?
   - Do we have a seat at the table for AI governance methodology?

## 8. Assessment & Pedagogical Implementation (University of Hohenheim)

### Assessment Dimensions: 8 Recommendations for Lecturers

**Core Challenge**: Traditional assessment methods (essays, take-home exams) become obsolete when ChatGPT can generate plausible text. Lecturers need to rethink both assessment design AND supervision/evaluation processes.

**8 Recommendations (Figure 11, Hohenheim)**:

1. **Design exams carefully with respect to new technological possibilities**
   - Closed-book exams (pen/paper or controlled environment without internet) are now relatively safer
   - For open-book exams with AI tools allowed: pre-test questions with ChatGPT, discard poor results, allow room for improvement
   - Add personal reflection requirements, focus on recent events (ChatGPT training data cutoff: Sept 2021), clarify which tools are allowed/forbidden

2. **Require students to declare how ChatGPT and other advanced tools were used**
   - Similar to academic integrity declarations in Cambridge University Press guidelines
   - Declarations should specify which steps involved ChatGPT (outline, proofreading, ideation, etc.)
   - Increases accountability, documents good faith use

3. **Innovate assessment formats beyond standardized written assignments**
   - Oral presentations, collaborative group projects, in-class assessments, creative formats (webpages, videos, animations)
   - Leverage ChatGPT's limited creativity: design assessments that require novel thinking beyond pattern replication
   - Assessment innovation requires additional lecturer time and staffing

4. **Rethink the supervision process for assignments**
   - No assignments without supervision (process documentation)
   - Require information on work steps during creation
   - Emphasize study design execution (especially empirical work)
   - Require declaration of ChatGPT use and purpose
   - Encourage oral presentations/defenses to verify mastery

5. **Rethink evaluation criteria for assignments**
   - Move beyond linguistic quality (ChatGPT excels here)
   - Focus on content quality, theoretical coherence, unique contributions, research alignment
   - Assess personal reflection and learning logs
   - Require proper references and argumentation structure

6. **Implement guidelines for avoiding plagiarism and copyright infringement**
   - Translate paraphrasing chains (English -> German -> Spanish -> English) complicate detection
   - Large language models may generate text identical to pre-existing sources by chance ("patchwork plagiarism")
   - AI Content Detectors currently only ~26% accurate; future tools may improve
   - Establish transparent university policy aligned with legal frameworks ("learningright" concept)
   - Lecturer responsibility: inform students of risks, encourage ethical use, implement detection guidelines

7. **Teach students how to use ChatGPT properly**
   - Develop subject-specific reflection on AI's impact
   - Create study programs on AI ethics, knowledge creation through AI systems
   - Redefine core competencies: which skills should students develop in presence of AI?
   - Promote equity and inclusion (ChatGPT can support students with writing/linguistic barriers)
   - Address bias awareness (ChatGPT trained on large datasets reflecting societal biases)

8. **Implement "Rules for Tools"** (governance framework)
   - General use allowed, subject to course requirements
   - Students accountable for achievements (AI can help but contains mistakes)
   - Mandatory disclosure of tools used, fields of application, prompts (where applicable)
   - Exceptions possible (prohibit in specific contexts, communicated in advance)

### Strategic Insight: Assessment as Transformation, Not Just Detection

The Hohenheim paper argues that assessment innovation is not about plagiarism detection, but about **transforming learning objectives and evaluation criteria** in light of AI capabilities. This is fundamentally an AI Literacy question: students must understand when AI is appropriate to use and when human expertise is required.

## 9. Outlook: Integrating ChatGPT into Institutional Practice

### The Inevitable Adoption Thesis

**Core premise** (Hohenheim): Universities cannot ignore that AI will be prevalent in daily life. The question is not whether to adopt AI, but how to do so responsibly.

**Technology momentum**:
- Rapid development of GPT-x models, multimodal interfaces, specialized tools
- Clear paths toward more potent generative AI models
- Integration across IT systems (Microsoft, Google, others embedding ChatGPT-like capabilities)
- Current ChatGPT is "only the first small step on a big road"

**Conversational agents as essential resource**: Lecturers and students should use generative AI tools, but with critical awareness and institutional guardrails.

### Equity & Access Challenges

**Premium vs. free tier divide**: ChatGPT Plus (20 USD/month) + OpenAI API pricing create unequal access. Universities must consider providing fee-based tools, free access, or institutional infrastructure to prevent inequality.

**Cost example**: 10,000 students at 20 USD/month = 2.4 million USD/year in aggregate. Decision required: who pays, and is universal access sustainable?

### Core Competencies in an AI-Augmented World

Students must develop **"robot-proof" skills** (Aoun 2018):
- Critical thinking: distinguish between AI-generated plausible content and verified fact
- Systems thinking: understand how AI systems work and their limitations
- Entrepreneurship: identify problems and create novel solutions (not in AI's training data)
- Cultural agility: apply knowledge to diverse contexts and ethical scenarios

**Critical thinking is non-negotiable**: As AI generates content more convincingly, the ability to critique, verify, and apply judgment becomes MORE essential, not less. Universities must actively teach this.

### Stakeholder Dialogue Required

**Eight stakeholder groups to involve**:
1. All faculties and disciplines (different traditions, requirements, opportunities)
2. IT/CS experts (research on generative AI, infrastructure, digital transformation)
3. Career centers and industry representatives (define required competencies)
4. Students (learning objectives, formats, conditions)
5. University didactics experts (pedagogical approaches, assessments)
6. Legal experts (examine licensing, regulations, institutional policies)
7. University administration (fair, efficient, high-quality teaching)
8. IT infrastructure departments (access, licensing, security, data protection)

**Multi-perspective dialogue** is critical because different groups have different legitimate concerns (compliance, pedagogy, costs, equity). Regulation cannot be designed by one function alone.

### Implementation Readiness

**Key barriers**:
- Assessment format change requires careful institutional planning (not overnight)
- Examination regulations must be revised formally
- Lecturers need time and support to adapt teaching/assessment
- Infrastructure investment may be required for institutional access

**Recommended approach**: Avoid **reactive prohibition** ("ChatGPT is forbidden"). Instead, adopt **proactive governance** ("Here's how to use ChatGPT responsibly in your course"), aligned with institutional values and legal compliance.

**Success looks like**: By 2027-2028, ChatGPT and similar tools are woven into teaching practice, students understand when/how to use them appropriately, institutional policies provide clarity, and critical thinking thrives alongside AI-enabled productivity.
