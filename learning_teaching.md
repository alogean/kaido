## 1. Scope & Mission

AI Buddy's Axis 2 is **Learning Support** : helping UZH students learn responsibly with AI, and helping UZH lecturers integrate genAI into teaching.

**Not** : a competitor to ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini.
**Yes** : an institutional layer that makes the responsible use of these tools meaningful in the UZH academic context (validated content, integration with UZH systems, equity of access, support to lecturers).

## 4. Discovery Approach (methodological debate)

Two complementary methods are on the table:

### Bottom-up , Focus Groups & User Testing (current default)
- Two focus groups held March 2026 (10 students), [user_testing_focus_group.md](user_testing_focus_group.md)
- Strengths : direct student voice, reveals priorities (learning > admin, peer pressure against AI in PhF)
- Limits (per Anna Schorn, May 2026, [input/learning_teaching/Strategie.pptx](../input/learning_teaching/Strategie.pptx)) :
  - Too slow versus the HS26 semester start window
  - Too open : students cannot describe value they cannot imagine
  - Risk of reproducing known answers, no differentiation from ChatGPT

### Top-down , Capability-First Discovery (proposed by Anna Schorn, May 2026)
Four-step sequence:
1. **Clarify internally** what AI Buddy can technically do that ChatGPT/Claude cannot (UZH-specific data, system integration, validated content, privacy).
2. **Derive 3 to 5 concrete use cases** where AI Buddy has a real advantage.
3. **Validate** those use cases with students and lecturers : *"is this relevant?"* not *"what do you want?"*.
4. **Implement.**

> Core principle : *"We must first know what we can offer, before we ask whether it is needed."*

### Working consensus
The two approaches are **complementary, not exclusive**. Focus groups remain useful at the **validation step (3)**, not as the entry point (1). The board's endorsed direction (D1 + D5) is closer to the top-down approach : a concrete pilot scope is defined first, then validated with motivated lecturers.

## 11. Workshop Framework , Decomposing the Opportunity Space

Structure designed to find white space at the intersection of AI Buddy + KlickerUZH + Lehrentwicklung + OLAT.

### 11.1 Two Personas

| Persona | Primary need | Key tension |
|---|---|---|
| **Student** | Reach learning outcomes with less friction and more agency | Too much AI = no learning; too little = falling behind peers already using ChatGPT |
| **Lecturer** | Deliver outcomes-aligned teaching at scale, with quality | Workload pressure vs pedagogical integrity in the AI era |

### 11.2 Educational psychology pillars

- **Constructive Alignment** (Biggs) , aligns learning outcomes, activities, and assessment
- **ICAP framework** (Chi 2014) , Interactive > Constructive > Active > Passive
- **Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan) , autonomy, competence, relatedness

### 11.3 Workshop flow (3 hours)

| Time | Phase | Output |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00,0:15 | Frame the question (board D1, D5; differentiator filters) | Shared frame |
| 0:15,0:45 | Persona deep-dive across 14 domains | Domain pain points |
| 0:45,1:30 | Use case generation (3-4 person groups, one domain each) | 30,60 raw use cases |
| 1:45,2:15 | Differentiation filter (F1 UZH knowledge / F2 system integration / F3 validated content / F4 privacy). Drop use cases scoring 0 | 15,25 survivors |
| 2:15,2:45 | 2x2 prioritization (impact vs effort) | 3,5 flagship use cases |
| 2:45,3:00 | Owner assignment & next steps | One-pager per flagship |

### 11.7 Glossary , Educational Psychology Concepts (psych anchors)

Brief definitions of the concepts cited in the domain anchors above. Use these to keep workshop participants on the same page when generating use cases.

**Active Learning** (Freeman et al., 2014) , Meta-analytic evidence that students in active-learning conditions outperform those in passive lectures. "Active" means students do something during the session (discuss, solve, write) rather than just listen.

**Cognitive Apprenticeship** (Collins, Brown & Newman, 1989) , Teaching complex cognitive skills by making expert thinking visible: modelling, coaching, scaffolding, articulation, reflection, exploration. Used wherever the goal is to develop expert-like reasoning.

**Cognitive Load Theory** (Sweller, 1988) , Learning is bounded by working memory. Distinguishes intrinsic load (task difficulty), extraneous load (poor design adds friction), and germane load (effort that builds long-term knowledge). Good teaching minimises extraneous load and protects germane load.

**Communities of Practice** (Wenger, 1998) , People who share a craft learn by belonging to a group that practises it together. Professional development happens through participation in the group, not only through formal instruction.

**Constructive Alignment** (Biggs, 1996) , A course design principle where intended learning outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment all target the same competencies. "Tell them what they should learn, teach them that, then test exactly that."

**Dual Coding** (Paivio, 1971) , Humans process information through two channels: verbal and visual. Combining well-chosen text and imagery improves recall and comprehension over text alone.

**Effective Feedback** (Hattie & Timperley, 2007) , Feedback works best when it answers three questions: *Where am I going?*, *How am I going?*, *Where to next?*. Most powerful at the task and process levels; least useful when aimed at the person ("you're smart").

**Five Phases of Assessment** (Teaching Tools framework) , The full lifecycle of an assessment: design, develop, deliver, grade, improve. Frames assessment as an iterative process, not a one-off event.

**Formative Assessment** (Black & Wiliam, 1998) , Assessment used *during* learning to diagnose and adjust, not to judge. Tight feedback loops between teacher and learner produce some of the largest measurable gains in learning outcomes.

**ICAP Framework** (Chi, 2014) , Classifies learning activities by cognitive depth: **I**nteractive > **C**onstructive > **A**ctive > **P**assive. The deeper the engagement, the more learning. Helps decide when AI assistance amplifies learning versus when it short-circuits it.

**Metacognitive Monitoring** (Flavell, 1979) , Awareness of one's own thinking and learning ("do I actually understand this?"). Students who monitor their comprehension learn more than those who don't, because they catch their own misunderstandings.

**Reflective Practice** (Schön, 1983) , Professionals develop by reflecting both *during* their actions (reflection-in-action) and *after* (reflection-on-action). Improvement comes from making tacit knowledge explicit and questioning it.

**Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan, 1985) , Motivation depends on three basic psychological needs: **autonomy** (sense of choice), **competence** (feeling capable), and **relatedness** (connection to others). Designs that support these three produce sustained engagement; designs that undermine them produce dropout.

**Self-Regulation / Goal-Setting** (Zimmerman, 2000) , Learners who set specific goals, monitor their progress, and adjust their strategies achieve more than those who do not. Self-regulation is a teachable skill, not a fixed trait.

**Social Constructivism / Zone of Proximal Development** (Vygotsky, 1978) , Knowledge is built through social interaction. The Zone of Proximal Development is the gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with help from a more capable peer or teacher , where the most productive learning happens.
